ISOM 835 PROJECT

Predictive Analytics: Macro-Economic Housing Forecast

Analyzing the S&P Case-Shiller Home Price Index via Federal Reserve Data.

Business Questions & Objectives

  • Primary Question: To what extent can the Federal Funds Rate predict changes in the S&P Case-Shiller Home Price Index?
  • Lag Analysis: Does a 3-month lead time in interest rate shifts provide a more accurate forecast for housing volatility?
  • Model Comparison: Which algorithm—Random Forest or Gradient Boosting—better captures the non-linear shifts in the current market?

Exploratory Data Analysis

Correlation between Fed Funds Rate and Home Price Index using a 3-month lag strategy.

Model Performance (R² Scores)

Gradient Boosting achieved a 0.7019 accuracy, identifying 70% of market variance.

Predictive Insights

Our analysis yielded a Gradient Boosting R² score of 0.7019, outperforming the Random Forest model (0.6928). This indicates that macro-economic indicators account for approximately 70% of housing price variance.

The remaining 30% suggests that "Hyper-Local" factors—such as inventory shortages in specific zip codes and regional migration patterns—remain significant drivers that federal policy cannot fully capture.

Ethical Interpretability & Bias

While a 70% accuracy rate is statistically significant, relying solely on macro-economic models for lending or investment can lead to "Algorithmic Redlining." By ignoring local socio-economic nuances, these models may unfairly devalue properties in emerging neighborhoods, inadvertently restricting credit access for minority homeowners.

We recommend using SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) to ensure model transparency and prevent reliance on proxy variables for protected demographic data.

Project Conclusion

This semester project demonstrates that while the Federal Reserve's "interest rate lever" is a powerful predictor of the U.S. housing market, it is not the sole determinant. The 0.70 R² score highlights the complexity of the 2026 real estate landscape, where federal policy and local demand intersect.
