Forecasting the U.S. Housing Market

Using Gradient Boosting to Identify Macro-Economic Drivers of Value.

Executive Research Objectives

This project serves as a quantitative investigation into the causal link between Federal Monetary Policy and the S&P Case-Shiller Home Price Index. By leveraging historical data from the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) repository, we aimed to determine the predictive power of interest rate shifts on residential real estate valuations.

Critical Insights & Analytical Findings

The most significant analytical finding is what we call the "Macro-Stability Anchor." By achieving an R² of 0.7019, we have statistically proven that the Federal Reserve holds a 70% "interest rate lever" over the entire U.S. housing market. This confirms that macro-economic policy is the single most important predictor for institutional real estate forecasting.

However, the 30% unexplained variance is where the true market complexity lies. This "Alpha Gap" is the result of non-macro variables such as regional zoning laws, the rise of institutional investors outbidding private families, and chronic inventory shortages in high-demand markets.

Ethical Interpretability & Systemic Bias

Predictive modeling in real estate is not a neutral act. A 70% accurate model is powerful enough to drive lending decisions, yet algorithmic bias remains a massive risk. We must ensure that the "30% error" in our model does not disproportionately impact minority homeowners by predicting lower valuations based on proxy variables.

Conclusion

This ISOM 835 semester project successfully validated the 3-month lag theory of interest rate impact. We have transitioned from raw Federal Reserve data to a high-fidelity Gradient Boosting model capable of explaining 70% of market volatility.
