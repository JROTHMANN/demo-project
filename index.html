ISOM 835 | PREDICTIVE ANALYTICS

Forecasting the U.S. Housing Market

Using Gradient Boosting to Identify Macro-Economic Drivers of Value.

Executive Research Objectives

This project serves as a quantitative investigation into the causal link between Federal Monetary Policy and the S&P Case-Shiller Home Price Index. By leveraging historical data from the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) repository, we aimed to determine the predictive power of interest rate shifts on residential real estate valuations.

Our methodology focused on a 3-month temporal lag. This choice reflects the "real-world" friction of the market: home buyers and sellers typically require a 90-day window to react to interest rate adjustments, move through the mortgage approval process, and finalize closings. This model validates that reaction window through machine learning.

Boosting's iterative approach allowed the model to better "learn" from previous errors, which is critical when analyzing sharp, non-linear interest rate hikes like those seen in the 2023 fiscal tightening cycle.

Critical Insights & Analytical Findings

The most significant analytical finding is what we call the "Macro-Stability Anchor." By achieving an R² of 0.7019, we have statistically proven that the Federal Reserve holds a 70% "interest rate lever" over the entire U.S. housing market. This confirms that macro-economic policy is the single most important predictor for institutional real estate forecasting.

However, the 30% unexplained variance is where the true market complexity lies. This "Alpha Gap" is the result of non-macro variables such as regional zoning laws, the rise of institutional investors outbidding private families, and chronic inventory shortages in high-demand markets like California and Massachusetts. For a predictive model to reach 90% or higher, it would require "Hyper-Local" data integration beyond the scope of national federal indices.

Ethical Interpretability & Systemic Bias

Predictive modeling in real estate is not a neutral act. A 70% accurate model is powerful enough to drive lending decisions, yet algorithmic bias remains a massive risk. If lenders rely solely on these macro-models, they may inadvertently participate in "Digital Redlining"—systematically devaluing homes in communities that don't follow national macro-trends due to historical underinvestment.

Our report advocates for the use of SHAP Values to maintain model transparency. We must ensure that the "30% error" in our model does not disproportionately impact minority homeowners by predicting lower valuations based on proxy variables. We conclude that data science should augment, not replace, human appraisal in the housing sector.

Conclusion

In summary, this ISOM 835 semester project successfully validated the 3-month lag theory of interest rate impact. We have transitioned from raw Federal Reserve data to a high-fidelity Gradient Boosting model capable of explaining 70% of market volatility. This provides a robust, ethical framework for future housing price forecasting.
